Problem Statement:

People need to cross the river and are unwilling to use a boat or to swim due to the need to move around goods quickly.

Design Statement:

Design, prototype, build and test a bridge that is both functional and economical using West Point Bridge Designer.

  • Constraints created by West Point Bridge Designer
  • Fost:
  • $250,000 or less: no points docked off
  • $400,000 - $250,000: 2 points docked off
  • $500,000 - $400,000: 4 points docked off
  • $500,000 or more: 6 points docked off
  • Functionality

Research Summary

Through two days of research, and an in depth investigation, we have decided what materials and bridge setup we should use. We found that both hollow members and quenched and tempered steel were effective in reducing size through their strenght, and thus reducing price. Additionally, hollow members increase the toughness of tensile steel per weight, making compressive forces less of a problem. We plan on using the 140 * 140 size for our members or a lower size, unless they are central to the design. We know that only members that are holding up a major amount of force will need more material. We also decided that we would want our elevation to be at lower height, like around 0 meters - 8 meters because we would require less members and joints thereby vastly reducing the cost. We know it will cost more to set up the bridge site, but we believe that we can compensate for the increase in price.

We also looked at many prominent modern bridge designs for inspiration in building bridges. Prototype “e” is base off of the age-old arch bridge and seeks to use its circular design for more support. Prototype “Z” is based off of todays most complex suspension bridges, with its single tower off of a pier, and ‘cables’ holding up the flexible bridge. Prototype “B” is an attempt to minimize the cost, regardless of the safety, and is loosely based off the first suspension bridges, which were made to be very flexible with rope and wood across jungle trees. Prototype “A” is a simple design and is not based on any bridges in the real world. Prototype “3” is based off of a combination of many existing bridges, but most significant due to its truss below the bridge, which allows it greater stability.

Cost is multiplied by two because of its importance in the project’s goals.

Safety: Safety is determined by the amount of bend by rank. The bridge that bends least gets the highest score, the next gets the next highest score, and so on.

Safety is multiplied by three because we believe it is very important to preserve life and broken bridges are expensive to repair.

Our final design choice is Prototype 3. It is high scoring in all categories, being under the price limit of $250,000 and very stable for its price. We like the idea of using a truss under the bridge, as we believe it partially negates the weight of the truss. Finally, we believe that we can expand on the design of Prototype 3 to make it much better and almost completely avoid bending on the bridge, while staying under the price limit of 250,000.

Modifications

After we had chose our main design, prototype 3, we had decided to reduce the number of beams and joints to reduce the cost. We took out some members and replaced all members with carbon steel. The cost did drop  around $15,000 but the bridge wasn’t as effective. It would often break at the member shown on the left. There would also be many members that were in great tension.

After our first modification we had decided to make it more symmetrical. We found out that part of the problem why the beam was breaking was (1) we needed a beam to hold up the middle, and (2) it wasn’t symmetrical. After we had done the modifications, the price only went up around $5,000 but it was worth the money because the bridge actually worked.

In this modification, we added more trusses around the bottom and sides to reinforce the bridge. We wanted more to worry about safety more than cost. We added more members at the bottom and the middle of the bridge to make sure it is stable. We also made some beams larger in order for the bridge not the bend and break. The cost did go up $10,000, but we were still under $250,000.

In this modification, we added more trusses to better distribute the weight of the bridge. However we quickly noticed that not all members were experiencing the same amount of force and elected to modify their sizes to compensate. This modification did increase the price a little but was offset by a decrease in member size.

In this modification we made the actual roadbed thicker, along with some of the larger and more important members. This helped stabilize the design. However, we are close to our budget limit of $250,000, so we plan to decrease the size of some less essential members in future iterations.

Final modification. In this modification, we added a truss on top for greater stability. We also removed two members from the bottom to fit into our price goal of 250,000. Finally, we added some more members to the center to make the bridge as stable as possible--it now hardly moves when crossed.

Final Design Solution

        The central idea of our final bridge design is reducing the bending of the bridge as much as possible while under the price of 250,000. We achieved this goal mostly because our truss was largely under, instead of over our bridge. For our setup, we decided to dig out as much as we could to allow a truss under our bridge. Therefore we dug out to 4 meters and added a pier for maximum support under our bridge. This configuration, while relatively expensive would give us just enough room and support to build an effective truss below the bridge. When building the truss, we were careful to reinforce the longer and more essential members including the ones marked on the diagram, and the members holding up the deck. This truss design reinforced members who were under the most tensile and compressive forces. It allowed the weight of the both truck and bridge to be evenly distributed throughout all the members to avoid bending or collapse. We used all quenched and tempered steel members because the superior material makes up for the price, as less material is required to perform the same task. As visible below, most of all of our members were hollowed out as well due to the reduce in cost, and increase in strength per weight. Additionally, most of our members were 110x110x5 mm in size, which further lowered our cost while the more important members were thicker from 150x150x7 size to 190x190x9. These thicker members were often the members with the greatest forces acting on them. Thus, the had to be larger in size to produce a sufficient internal reaction force.

        Our bridge accomplished all of our goals. We were able to achieve highly stable design just under the price of $250,000 as our bridge costed $249,841.37. We believe that a bridge must be both safe and cost-effective and showcase this bridge as effective in both regards.

Design Brief:

Designer:

Aviram Bhalla-Levine

Client:

TravelDreidel

Targeted Audience:

Children under the age of ten and those who are children at heart.

Problem Statement:

Playing the Dreidel game can be difficult on the go, requiring a flat surface and chocolate coins to act as gelt. Customers are demanding a way to play the Dreidel game without any physical components.

Design Statement:

Design, prototype, build, and test an electronic dreidel based on integrated circuits and wiring components.

Constraints:

The system must have AOI logic gate incorporated in it.

The system must have at least three inputs per person.

The system must have a clear purpose and use.

Deliverables:

An actual, functional circuit.

A simulation that verifies the functionality of the circuit.

A completed documentation of the design process that includes:

A title page with an image of the completed circuit,

An accurate table of contents with all pages listed,

A design brief that details constraints and deliverables,

Design specifications and truth tables that explain the relation between the inputs and the outputs,

The output logic expression and simplification,

A detailed documentation of the final iteration of the circuit and multisim.

Design Specifications

The inputs of the function are not actually switches but are rather the values outputted by the D-Flip-Flop counter, represented by the hebrew letters נ ג ה שׁ. These values drive the outputs of the function, which are a representation of these hebrew letters on two seven-segment-displays that have been oriented horizontally. This system simulates the spinning of a dreidel, which randomly falls during its spin resulting in one of these letters being physically displayed. Thus certain lights on the seven segment displays are made to light up in accordance with the hebrew letters that they represent.

Although the original idea was to also include the players’ values of gelt in the form of single LEDs on a different side of the breadboard, a lack of time has not allowed the completion of this ambition. Players may still choose to alter the LEDs that track the count of gelt by hand in accordance with the current hebrew letter displayed, although this method does not ensure a lack of cheating by players as a more digitized method of keeping score might.

Because the player has little direct control over the inputs, there are instead two functions interact with the circuit:

Spin Dreidel: Pressing this button begins the cycling through on the D-Flip-Flop of the four hebrew letters. Releasing this button stops the cycling and shows a single letter.

Reset Game: This switch resets the players’s gelt and the pot’s gelt count to two, effectively resetting the game.

Given more time, the circuit would have included additional switches to represent which player was actively taking his or her turn.

Final Design Description

As a whole, the final design at the moment is a great success. The current circuit solves most of the problem as described in the design brief: it allows dreidel enthusiasts to easily play the dreidel game through simulated dreidel spins and manual scorekeeping without the need for a stable surface to play on. The circuit effectively solves the design problem and could be readily fashioned into a printed circuit board for mass assembly.

Currently, the breadboard is split into two sections. Wiring that is related to the randomization of the hebrew letters is located on the left while wiring associate with players score or gelt is located on the right. If the circuit was fully functional as intended, the center area would have additional logic ICs that would facilitate communication between the roll of the dreidel and the pot of gelt of the players.

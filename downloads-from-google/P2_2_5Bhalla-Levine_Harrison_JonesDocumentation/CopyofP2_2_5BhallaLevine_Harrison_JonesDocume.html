Target Consumer: Those wondering whether or not they are pirates.

Problem Statement:

People everywhere are curious about whether or not they are in fact pirates . . .

Design Statement:

We must create a digital sign using seven segment displays that show the phrase, “YAr HAr You Are A Pirate.”

Constraints:

  • There must be 2 inputs utilized for a total of 4 states shown on a given display.
  • Each person must create outputs for at least 2 displays.
  • Any 1 display may not be dark for more than 2 states.

MULTISIM:

Overview

For A Video of the Completed CDS, Please Click HERE

For the multisim file, after each individual created their CDS files for their assigned columns, I went through, found duplicates of logic expressions, and ensured each seven segment was wired with the universal gates that were most efficient. Only one segment was more efficient with NOR gates, and the other five segments were more efficient with NAND.

To ensure I created each gate correctly, I created the UG logic for each expression used and checked for the proper outputs using probes. If the UG logic was correct I would create the next one until I had only to wire the seven segments to the associated UG gates.

CDS Efficiency

The final CDS file used a total of:

  • 3 NOR gates
  • 3 of 4 available NOR gates on 1 IC = (3 gates / 4 gates) * 100 = 75% Efficiency
  • 21 NAND gates
  • 5 ICs with 4 of the 4 available gates used & 1 IC with 1 of 4 available gates used

 = (21 gates / 24 gates) * 100 = 87.5% Efficiency

Final Efficiency

(NOR + NAND / 2)

(75 + 87.5) / 2

81.25% Efficiency

Our final design solution is a prototype of a marketable circuit that could be sold to people who are wondering if they are indeed pirates. Our final prototype contains three main sections of interest on the breadboard. In the first section, “logic,” a single D-Flip-Flop integrated circuit is used to generate a binary count from zero to three. Four NAND integrated circuits are used to process the resultant binary X and Y inputs and convert them into expressions for each of the seven segment displays light emitting diodes to display four different, sequential messages that when read together express the phrase, “YAr HAr You Are A Pirate” This is an extremely efficient way of converting sequential binary into logic expressions as NAND gates require fewer actual transistors than AOI gates and because we have simplified our logic implementations for NAND through the elimination of redundant double NAND inversions. In our initial sketches of our circuits in AOI, NAND, and NOR implementations, we found that AOI was clearly far less efficient than NAND or NOR,  so we decided not to use it. We were initially unsure if NAND or NOR was superior but we decided not to use NOR gates in our actual implementation after finding that they did not always output the correct output when given a certain input; when two low values were imputed, we expected a high value but received a low one. Due to the reuse of certain parts of logic implementations in other implementations in the breadboard, our breadboard is much more efficient than our simulation. Our breadboard’s implementation uses fourteen out of sixteen available NAND gates, with only one integrated circuit having some unused gates, yielding a 87.5% efficiency. There is an additional NAND gate on the board. However, it is not actually in use and we have not factored it into this calculation. In the second section, “resistors and distribution,” all of the logic expressions received from the first section are connected to a single 300Ω resistor and then connected by direct wire to their appropriate seven segment display pin. This allows for efficient resistor use to save money on parts and avoids inadvertently touching open resistor wires. The third section, “display,” contains six common cathode seven segment displays, which display their sequential message, “YAr HAr / You / Are A / Pirate.”
